    @paragraph: Quantum computing is a new paradigm in computation that leverages the principles of quantum mechanics to solve problems that are currently intractable for classical computers. Unlike classical computers, which store information as binary digits bits, quantum computers use quantum bits qubits that can exist in multiple states simultaneously, allowing them to perform many calculations in parallel.
    
    @section: What is Quantum Computing?
    
    @paragraph: At its core, quantum computing seeks to exploit the peculiarities of quantum mechanics, such as superposition and entanglement, to solve complex computational problems. Superposition allows quantum computers to represent multiple possible solutions at once, while entanglement enables qubits to influence each other instantaneously, regardless of distance. These properties provide a significant speedup for certain types of calculations.
    
    @section: History of Quantum Computing
    
    @paragraph: The concept of quantum computing dates back to the 1980s, when physicist Richard Feynman proposed that quantum systems could be simulated on quantum computers, since classical computers struggle to simulate quantum phenomena efficiently. Since then, researchers have made tremendous progress in the field, from the development of quantum algorithms to the construction of prototype quantum computers by companies like IBM, Google, and Rigetti.

    @chapter: Applications of Quantum Computing
    
    @chapter: Cryptography
    
    @paragraph: One of the most well known applications of quantum computing is in the field of cryptography. Shors algorithm, a quantum algorithm developed by Peter Shor in 1994, demonstrated that quantum computers could efficiently factor large numbers, which is the basis for breaking RSA encryption. This poses a significant threat to the security of digital communications. However, quantum safe encryption methods are being developed to address this concern.

    @paragraph: Despite the immense potential of quantum computing, there are significant challenges to overcome. These include issues related to qubit coherence, error correction, and scalability. Current quantum computers are prone to errors due to decoherence, a phenomenon where quantum states lose their information. Researchers are actively working on developing quantum error correction codes and more stable qubits to make quantum computers practical for real world applications.
